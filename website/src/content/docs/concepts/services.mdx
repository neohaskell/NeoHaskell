## What You Might Expect

- "Event sourcing" sounds like it means everything is asynchronous and eventually consistent
- Microservices mean every service has its own database and communicates via message queues
- Services sound like DDD jargon — complex, theoretical, hard to apply
- You're worried that adopting event sourcing means accepting eventual consistency everywhere

## Why That Breaks

- Not every part of your system needs eventual consistency — some things need to be strongly consistent
- A bank transfer must be atomic: debit and credit happen together or not at all
- If you make everything eventually consistent, you lose the ability to enforce invariants across operations
- The core problem: "event sourcing" doesn't mean "eventual consistency everywhere" — it means choosing the right consistency model for each part of your system

## Services: The Mental Model Shift

- A **service** is an independent model with its own events, commands, and updates
- Within a service, consistency is strong — the entity enforces invariants
- Across services, consistency is eventual — services communicate via events
- The boundary is where you choose: "inside here, we're strongly consistent; outside, we're eventually consistent"
- NeoHaskell's services define these boundaries — the language enforces them

### The Rosetta Stone

| What you'd say | Banking term | NeoHaskell term | What it actually is |
|---------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| "Retail vs. Lending" | Business division | Service | An independent model with its own events |
| "My account" | Account | Entity | A consistency boundary within a service |
| "Transfer between services" | Inter-division settlement | Cross-service event | An event that crosses a service boundary |

<Aside type="note" title="Start as a monolith">
NeoHaskell services are deployment boundaries, not code boundaries. You can start with all services in a single deployment (monolith) and split them into separate deployments when you actually need to — no rearchitecture required. The code doesn't change; only the deployment configuration does.
</Aside>

## What Happens When Retail Talks to Lending

- A customer applies for a loan — this crosses from the Retail service to the Lending service
- The Retail service publishes a `LoanApplicationSubmitted` event
- The Lending service subscribes to this event and creates a `LoanApplication` entity in its own model
- The Lending service has its own events: `LoanApproved`, `LoanDisbursed`, `LoanRepaid`
- These events are meaningless in the Retail service — they use different language, different rules
- The boundary is explicit: Retail doesn't know about loan approval logic; Lending doesn't know about account balances

### Consistency Within vs. Across Services

- **Within Retail**: a deposit and a balance check are strongly consistent — the entity enforces this
- **Across Retail and Lending**: a loan disbursement and an account credit are eventually consistent — they happen via events
- The choice is explicit: you decide where the boundary is, and NeoHaskell enforces it

## In NeoBank Terms

- NeoBank has two services: **Retail** (checking/savings accounts) and **Lending** (loans)
- Retail has its own events: `AccountOpened`, `MoneyDeposited`, `MoneyWithdrawn`
- Lending has its own events: `LoanApplicationSubmitted`, `LoanApproved`, `LoanDisbursed`
- A transfer within Retail is strongly consistent — the entity enforces the debit and credit atomically
- A loan disbursement that credits a Retail account is eventually consistent — it crosses the boundary via an event
- The two services can be deployed as one unit (monolith) or two units (microservices) — the code is identical

### When to Split Services

- Split when the services have genuinely different consistency requirements
- Split when the services have different scaling requirements
- Split when the services have different teams or deployment cycles
- Don't split prematurely — a monolith with clear service boundaries is easier to operate than microservices

- **Service definition**: a NeoHaskell service defines a boundary — its own events, commands, entities, and updates
- **Cross-service communication**: services communicate via events — one service publishes, another subscribes
- **Consistency model**: within a service, strong consistency; across services, eventual consistency
- **Deployment flexibility**: start as a monolith, split into microservices when needed — one configuration change
- **Language boundaries**: each service uses its own ubiquitous language — "account" means different things in Retail and Lending

## How NeoHaskell Enforces This

- Services define boundaries — the compiler enforces that cross-service access is explicit
- Cross-service events are typed — you can't accidentally use a Retail event in a Lending handler
- The `withOutbound` integration pattern makes cross-service communication explicit in the type
- Starting as a monolith and splitting into microservices requires only a configuration change — no code changes
